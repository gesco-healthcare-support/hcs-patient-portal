## Industry / regulatory context

- [17 U.S.C. § 101 -- Definitions (Cornell LII)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/101) -- Work-made-for-hire has nine enumerated categories; **commissioned software is NOT one of them**. Contractor-written software is not WMFH by default.
- [17 U.S.C. § 201 -- Ownership of copyright](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/201) -- Default rule: authorship (and ownership) vests in the contractor absent written assignment.
- [U.S. Copyright Office Circular 30 -- Works Made for Hire](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) -- Official guidance confirming contractor software requires express written assignment, not just WMFH language.
- [Cal. Labor Code § 2870](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=2870) -- Governs employee invention assignments; explicitly does not apply to independent contractors, so any prior Gesco contractor needed a separate IP-assignment clause.
- [California SOS Business Search (bizfile)](https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/search) -- Primary check for Gesco's registered entity and any sibling holding a latent SOW.
- [ABA Business Law Today -- IP Due Diligence: Patent Ownership and Title (2025)](https://businesslawtoday.org/2025/03/intellectual-property-due-diligence-review-patent-ownership-title/) -- Audit chain-of-title and catch gaps from un-assigned contractor work.
- [Practical Law -- Software, Cloud & IT Due Diligence Checklist](https://content.next.westlaw.com/practical-law/document/If28f9ecb0fbe11e698dc8b09b4f043e0/Software-Cloud-Other-IT-Due-Diligence-in-M-A-Transactions-Checklist) -- Standard M&A IP-diligence checklist; "prior contractor assignment" is always Item 1.
- [CCBJ -- Work For Hire Doctrine Almost Never Works In Software Contracts](https://ccbjournal.com/articles/work-hire-doctrine-almost-never-works-software-development-contracts) -- Why the belt-and-suspenders ("WMFH plus present-tense assignment") clause is the industry default.
- [Farella Braun + Martel -- Employment Law Issues in Contractor WMFH Clauses](https://www.fbm.com/publications/employment-law-issues-to-consider-before-including-work-made-for-hire-clauses-in-contractor-agreements/) -- California wrinkle: a bare WMFH clause can reclassify a contractor as an employee for tax purposes.
- [Black Duck -- M&A Software Due Diligence Checklist (eBook)](https://www.blackduck.com/resources/ebooks/software-due-diligence.html) -- Industry-standard handover checklist; core item: obtain copies of all SOWs, MSAs, and contractor agreements.

## Likely answer

**HIGH confidence**: No active external client contract exists for this codebase. Gesco is the internal owner/sponsor. The prior developer was either (a) a Gesco employee or (b) an external contractor whose IP was assigned to Gesco via a contractor agreement. No third-party SOW in scope.

**Assumptions that would flip this:**
- Discovery of an unsigned or silent IP-assignment clause for a past contractor (creates chain-of-title gap under 17 USC 201).
- Any invoice, email, or master-services reference naming an outside client as the commissioning party.
- Any co-branding or white-label hint (there is none in repo).

## Recommended approach to obtain a definitive answer

- Ask Gesco principals (business owner + manager): "Who originally commissioned this codebase? Was it built for Gesco internally, or for a client?"
- Ask HR / finance: "Do we have a signed IP-assignment agreement on file for the prior developer? W-2 employee or 1099 contractor?"
- If contractor: obtain the executed contractor agreement and confirm present-tense copyright assignment ("hereby assigns"), not a future promise.
- If no written assignment exists: get a post-hoc IP-assignment signed by the prior developer before first deploy.

## Default assumption if unanswered

Treat the codebase as Gesco-owned, internally sponsored, no active external client. Proceed with development. Flag IP-assignment verification as a pre-deploy blocker.

## Risk if assumption is wrong

If a prior contractor never assigned copyright and the WMFH clause is ineffective (software is not in the nine § 101(2) categories), Gesco could be deploying software it does not own. A disgruntled prior dev could assert copyright, demand licence fees, or enjoin deployment. Cheap to fix now (post-hoc assignment letter); expensive to fix after a commercial dispute.
